Common Equine Emergencies

Conditions that require immediate veterinary intervention.

Severe Colic

Pawing, rolling, sweating, looking at the flank, or elevated heart rate. Do not wait if symptoms are severe.

Acute Lameness

Non-weight bearing on any limb, suspected fractures, or signs of acute laminitis (founder).

Deep Lacerations

Heavy bleeding, wounds near joints, or puncture wounds to the hoof or body cavity.

Eye Injuries

Squinting, tearing, swollen eyelids, or cloudiness in the eye. Eye issues deteriorate rapidly.

What To Do Before We Arrive

Your actions in the crucial moments before the vet arrives can make a significant difference in the outcome.

For Colic

Remove all feed and hay. Walk the horse slowly if they are trying to roll violently, but allow them to rest if they are quiet. Do NOT administer medication unless instructed by the vet.

For Bleeding

Apply firm, continuous pressure with a clean towel or bandage. Do not peek to see if bleeding has stopped. Keep the horse calm and still.

For Eye Issues

Place the horse in a dark stall if possible. Do NOT try to pry the eyelids open or force medication into the eye.
